Overview

There are many crimes committed in New York everyday. There are also many dogs in the city. You'd think people are less inclined to commit crimes if they are happy in their lives, and puppies notoriously make people happy.

The correlation between the two data sets is analysed in this extremely useful website and the results are in: shockingly, when more puppies are born, the crime rates go up. But we can just ignore this and keep getting pups.

Crime

New York city can be a very dangerous area, with an average of 200 murders per year. Data was collected and analyzed in terms of frequency, location and category.

The data on crime is extensive and API was used to collect data over a period of two years (2013-2015). This allowed us to develop a thorough understanding of the patterns.

Puppies

Puppies are an amazing addition to our lives, there should be more of them! Data was collected on the birthrate of puppies as well as their names, breeds and locations.

This data is all collected here and is plotted to let you examine the results, pick which breed you should get and what unique (or generic) name to give your pupper.

Analysis

Surprisingly there is some vague correlation between the number of puppies born and the number of crimes committed. Remember correlation does not always equal causation!

The data set were compared in terms of location, time and the ratio of genders. Each of these elements show that the results pretty much match up.

More puppies = more crime.

Future

There are many uses to the data if more were to be collected and analyzed. The datasets together can suggest to you places you should live (places with more puppies, or with less crime). It also suggests that unfortunately we should get less puppies as this may correlate to less crime occuring.

Individually the datasets can help you pick a pet, name your pet and when to get your pet; as well as when not to go outside (to not risk being the victim of crime), and which days of the week are safest. More data could be collected about different cities, times and types of pet to expand this dataset.

Correlation does not equal causation.

This plot shows normalized data for number of crimes commited over the two year period, and the number of puppies born.

There's correlation!

The graph shows how the two lines follow the same seasonal changes and correlations. The crime data is very noisy, but the puppy data seems to higlight the trend. The value curve up towards the middle of the year and drop down towards the end of the year. Between 2015 and 2016 the values flatten out. There is even correlation between the dip that seemed to happen around June 2013. If correlation were to equal causation, then it would be suggested that we should reduce the number of puppies being born and this would also decrease the amount of crime. However, this is not the case and we certainly wouldn't want a world with less puppies.
